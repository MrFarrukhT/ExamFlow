Tara Redman – Chocolate Maker

Three years ago, I was a manager for a hotel chain. Working in a busy, fast-moving industry was tiring, but also exciting in a way. But one day, I decided to leave and start my own chocolate business. I'd always enjoyed making things using my own ideas, and realised I couldn't earn money from this unless I was prepared to do it full-time. So I left the hotel and began working for myself.

Because I had some savings, I decided not to ask the bank for money. For the first three months, I made chocolates in my tiny little kitchen at home, and took samples to local shops and restaurants for customers to try. It was quite a worrying time: as my savings began to run out, I started to regret not getting a loan from the bank. But my chocolates were hand-made from the finest ingredients, and I never had any doubts that they'd sell in the end. And they did: within six months I had plenty of orders.

I now have an assistant who makes the chocolates while I deal with the business side of things. I still make them myself occasionally, but not as often as I'd like to. I don't go on business trips to five-star hotels any more, though to be honest, even that got dull after a while. These days, I'm never sure how much I'll earn in a month, or even what I'll be doing the next day, but that's all part of the fun. I sometimes wish I still had a bunch of workmates to hang out with, but otherwise I don't regret my decision for a moment.

When we think of sharks, we often imagine fierce, meat-eating creatures. This might not be the whole picture though. Bonnetheads are the second smallest sharks in the hammerhead shark family. They can be found off the coasts of North and South America, in places where the water isn't too deep and is therefore warmer.

Samantha Leigh, a student at the University of California, became interested in the diet of bonnethead sharks. 17 Previously, it was thought that the sharks only did this accidentally, when they were hunting for fish hiding amongst the plants.

Leigh wanted to know more about how well plants provide bonnetheads with what they need to survive and grow. To try and find out, Leigh, together with a team of researchers, started by growing their own seagrass. 18 Then, in the next part of the process, they fed a group of five bonnetheads a diet that was 90% seagrass and 10% meat, for three weeks. During the experiment, the sharks all gained weight. 19

The team believe their discovery will change what scientists think about how sharks feed. 20 And we must work to ensure the protection of all ocean plant life, not just seagrass, as we may well discover that many other sea creatures also depend on plants for food. The bonnethead is the first example of a shark that eats both meat and plants, but it may not be the last.
