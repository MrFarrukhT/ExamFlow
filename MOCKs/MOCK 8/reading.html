The Terracotta Army

  In March 1974, a group of peasants digging for a well in the Shaanxi Province of China unearthed fragments of a clay figure – the first evidence of what would turn out to be a remarkable historical discovery. This find was none other than part of an army of thousands of terracotta warriors and horses, which had been buried for more than 2,000 years.

  The terracotta statues were discovered approximately 1.6 kilometres to the east of the Mausoleum of Emperor Qin Shi Huang, the first emperor to unify China. They had been buried with him in order to protect his rule in the afterlife. The actual tomb of Qin Shi Huang has never been opened and there are no plans to excavate it in the near future.

  The peasant who discovered the pieces, Yang Zhifa, was immediately sent to see a government archaeologist, Zhao Kangmin. Following an investigation by Zhao and some of his colleagues, the dig was expanded. The Chinese government recognised the significance of the discovery and sent a team of archaeologists under Professor Yuan Zhongyi, accompanied by archaeologists from the Museum of the Terracotta Warriors and Horses and more than two thousand local farmers, to assist with the excavation. A vast amount of hard work followed, and a protective building was erected over the site, which opened to the public in October 1979.

  Archaeologists have established that there are three pits at the site. They are located next to one another, with Pit 1 being the largest, covering an area of over 14,000 square metres and containing around six thousand figures. Pit 2, discovered in 1976 a short distance to the north of Pit 1, has yielded precious relics, including a kneeling archer and a cavalry horse, and is thought to contain as many as 1,300 warriors and horses. However, the excavation of Pit 2 has not yet been completed. Pit 3 is the smallest and appears to be the command centre of the terracotta army, as most of the warriors are officers. Pit 3 has also produced a total of 68 warriors, a war chariot, and four horses.

  While there were originally plans to dig a Pit 4, a decision was taken not to proceed with this, as it was found to be an unfinished pit which was empty.

  All together, the site contains an astonishing 8,000 warriors, 670 horses and more than 100 chariots. Each individual figure was created as a unique work of art. There are different types of soldiers – officers at different levels of rank, archers and infantrymen, along with horses and chariots. They stand at around 1.8 metres in height, with the commanders being somewhat taller. They have been made with incredible attention to detail, with hair, facial features, clothing, shoes and even the patterns on the soles of shoes all modelled individually. The warriors were originally painted in bright colours, but when they were exposed to the air, these colours began to fade. Research continues into how to preserve the figures better in the future.

  The warrior statues are arranged in battle formation and stand facing to the east, looking ready to defend their Emperor. They seem to represent a ceremonial guard, and yet it is thought that they may also be an actual representation of the army of Qin Shi Huang. Unfortunately, no written historical records describing the terracotta figures have ever been discovered, and historians and archaeologists have had to rely on clues from the site itself. But one thing is certain: the terracotta army is an impressive achievement in ancient art and archaeology and continues to fascinate people around the world.

  In 1987, the Mausoleum of the First Qin Emperor, including the terracotta army, was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The site is now one of the most popular tourist attractions in China, welcoming millions of visitors every year.

How staying cool changed the USA

A  In the 1920s and 1930s, before air conditioning became a widespread reality, people attempted to keep cool in summer by various methods – sleeping outside on porches, sitting on fire escapes and, if they were wealthy, decamping to the mountains or the sea shore. But the air conditioning industry grew rapidly in the 1930s, and from 1942, when the federal government prohibited the installation of air conditioning in new buildings for the duration of World War II, to 1946, the last full year of that ban, orders for air conditioning equipment rose by 300 per cent. Then, as postwar prosperity took hold, air conditioning spread rapidly throughout American life, altering social habits, employment patterns, and even politics.

B  The term 'air conditioning' was coined by a textile mill engineer named Stuart W. Cramer, but it was Willis Carrier, from Buffalo, New York, USA, who became known as the 'father of air conditioning'. Carrier, who was commissioned by a publishing company to devise a system that would keep temperature and humidity at comfortable levels during the summer, came up with a method for cooling and drying the air. By 1902, he had invented the modern air conditioner, although his apparatus was too large and expensive for anyone but industries and large institutions to afford.

C  During the 1920s, air conditioning units were installed in such varied institutions as theatres, department stores, hotels, trains, and ocean liners. Although it is hard to know how much influence air conditioning had on American cinema habits, there is little question that theatre owners at first promoted air conditioning more than the films. And air conditioning contributed mightily to the growth of the so-called 'sun belt', which stretches from Southern California to Florida, which would not have grown at the pace it did in the twentieth century without the cooling benefits brought by air conditioning. Nowadays, while there are still some cities where daily business closes down after lunch in hot weather, Americans have become so used to air conditioning that few people remember or can even imagine living without it.

D  Air conditioning's impact on American society has not been entirely positive. Marsha Ackermann, a historian at the University of Michigan, USA, has argued that air conditioning has contributed to the placidity of the American populace in summer. And there can be no denying that air conditioning sealed off Americans indoors, and that suburban design resulted in fewer porches and sidewalk cafes. Summer life became more private as people stopped sitting out of doors. There was less strolling on the street in the evening because people were in their homes or in air-conditioned cinemas. And although air conditioning made indoor work environments more tolerable, it also made people less tolerant of heat, particularly as lightweight clothes gave way to business suits that could only be worn indoors. It seems quite likely that without air conditioning, Americans would be more physically fit and perhaps less obese.

E  There have also been concerns about the environmental costs of air conditioning. Since the 1970s, scientists have expressed worries about the depletion of the ozone layer in the Earth's upper atmosphere by the chemicals in the air conditioning units themselves. There have been ongoing efforts to develop more environmentally friendly systems, but there are also concerns about the level of energy consumed, and the consequent effects on climate change.

F  Air conditioning has had an intriguing impact on American electoral politics. Historically, the Democratic Party in the USA did especially well in the more liberal Northeast and West Coast, while the more conservative Republican Party did well in the South. However, in recent decades, as millions of people migrated from the Northeast and Midwest to the sun belt states in the South and Southwest, especially after air conditioning made those areas bearable in summer, patterns began to shift. A number of sun belt states became more Republican. For instance, Arizona became a heavily Republican state from the 1960s onwards, as the population moved in and the economy grew around Phoenix. The movement of retirees to the sun belt, especially to Florida, has also had a significant political impact.

G  Carrier once wrote that air conditioning would revolutionise civilisation. Many critics have dismissed this claim as exaggeration, but there is no doubt that the technology did, in fact, help to change American society significantly. By the 1980s, air conditioning could be found in 70 per cent of all American homes, offices, and automobiles. It fostered industrial development, promoted population migration, and transformed American culture – not just geographically but also in terms of lifestyle and social behaviour. Whatever the future may hold, the air conditioner has firmly established itself as one of the most consequential inventions of the twentieth century.

Review: Superman on the Couch: What Superheroes Really Tell Us about Ourselves and Our Society

  Danny Fingeroth's book Superman on the Couch: What Superheroes Really Tell Us about Ourselves and Our Society is one of the first serious studies of the superhero genre in popular culture. Fingeroth, a comic book writer, spent decades working for Marvel Comics, and knows the genre from the inside. As a result, his analysis is informed by years of experience in creating superhero stories, and he writes with the authority of someone who understands how these characters function in the larger cultural imagination.

  The book begins by asking what makes someone a superhero. Fingeroth argues that a superhero is defined by several key qualities. First, the superhero must have extraordinary abilities that set them apart from ordinary people. Second, there must be a 'mission' – superheroes are not simply powerful; they choose to use their powers in the service of others, often at great personal cost. Third, there must be a dual identity, which creates narrative tension and allows the character to move between the everyday world and the world of heroic adventure. Finally, Fingeroth emphasises that superheroes generally represent good, upholding justice and fighting evil.

  One of Fingeroth's most interesting arguments is that superheroes are essentially immortal. Unlike characters in most forms of fiction, who experience genuine change and often meet their end within the narrative, superheroes exist in a kind of perpetual present. Batman and Superman have been fighting crime for over 70 years, and while their stories may be revised or reimagined, the characters themselves never truly age or die. This permanence, Fingeroth suggests, is part of their appeal. In an uncertain world, superheroes provide a reassuring sense of continuity.

  The book's most compelling section is its analysis of Spider-Man, a character Fingeroth worked on extensively during his time at Marvel. Spider-Man, Fingeroth argues, revolutionised the genre by introducing the concept of the 'superhero with problems'. Unlike earlier heroes such as Superman, who was virtually invincible, Spider-Man was vulnerable, both physically and emotionally. He struggled with money, relationships, and guilt, particularly over the death of his Uncle Ben. This made Spider-Man far more relatable to readers, especially young people who saw their own anxieties reflected in the character.

  Fingeroth also explores why superheroes are almost exclusively American in origin. He attributes this to the unique circumstances of American history and culture. The United States, as a nation built by immigrants, has always celebrated the idea of reinvention – the notion that anyone can become something greater than their origins suggest. This, Fingeroth argues, is the essence of the superhero narrative: an ordinary person (or in Superman's case, an immigrant from another planet) transforms into something extraordinary. The superhero embodies the American Dream.

  However, the book is not without its weaknesses. At times, Fingeroth's analysis feels superficial. For instance, his discussion of gender and superheroes is disappointingly brief. While he acknowledges that most superheroes are male and that female superheroes have historically been marginalised or sexualised, he does not delve deeply into why this is the case or how it reflects broader societal attitudes toward gender. Similarly, his treatment of race in superhero comics is cursory. These are significant omissions in a book that purports to reveal 'what superheroes really tell us about ourselves and our society'.

  Another limitation is that Fingeroth focuses almost entirely on mainstream superhero comics published by Marvel and DC. He gives little attention to alternative or independent superhero narratives, which often challenge the conventions of the genre. By concentrating on the most commercially successful examples, Fingeroth presents a somewhat narrow view of what superheroes can be and what they can represent.

  Despite these shortcomings, Superman on the Couch remains a valuable contribution to the study of popular culture. Fingeroth writes with clarity and passion, and his insights into the psychology of superhero narratives are often illuminating. He demonstrates convincingly that superheroes are not simply escapist fantasies, but reflections of our deepest hopes, fears, and values. For anyone interested in understanding why superheroes continue to dominate contemporary entertainment, this book is an essential starting point.
