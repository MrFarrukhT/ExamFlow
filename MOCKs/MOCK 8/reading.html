The Terracotta Army

  It was a prolonged drought in 1974 that forced farmer Yang Zhifa and his five brothers to start digging a well in a small wooded area just outside their village in Lingtong County near the city of Xi'an in Northwest China, eventually reaching a depth of 15 metres. On March 29th, they found something much more remarkable than water - the terracotta head of a Chinese warrior along with a heavily-corroded bronze arrowhead. They did not understand the significance of what they had found at the time, but the brothers had stumbled across the first emperor of China's Terracotta Army.

  When local archaeologist and museum curator Zhao Kangmin got a phone call a couple of weeks later about the farmers' discovery, he knew he needed to get down to the dig site as quickly as possible. The farmers weren't archaeologists, and he was worried they would unwittingly damage the site as they continued to dig for treasure. Zhao jumped on his bicycle and raced to the site. Once there, his worst fears were realised. The brothers had indeed continued to dig, and the site was now littered with hundreds of fragments of shattered terracotta. He told the farmers that they had to stop what they were doing immediately. They were reluctant to agree, but finally did so.

  Zhao gathered the broken fragments together and loaded them onto a truck to transport them to the Lingtong County Museum where he worked. Over the course of the next few days, Zhao painstakingly put the fragments back together. When he was finished, two imposing, 1.78-metre-tall figures stood before him. This was an incredible find, but he wondered if it was just the tip of the iceberg. To find out, he assigned a small team to start work on excavating the site. It soon became clear, as more soil was cleared and more figures and fragments of figures emerged, that far from there being just a few figures buried underground, there were potentially hundreds.

  Zhao was uncertain whether the government in Beijing would allocate resources to a large-scale excavation and decided to continue the work with a small team. However, when a journalist visiting the area heard about his work and published an article about the site, word travelled fast. The authorities were quick to recognise the importance of the find and ordered a full excavation of the site. Soon, the dig was teeming with archaeologists. It was quickly dawning on everyone that the farmers had stumbled upon one of the most astonishing archaeological finds in modern history. What they had found was an army cast in terracotta - made to defend the first emperor of China, Qin Shi Huang, in the afterlife.

  Over the months that followed, four pits were uncovered. To the astonishment of everyone involved, what was unearthed was an entire army, complete with horses and chariots. The first pit revealed the main bulk of the emperor's army - six thousand warriors in total arranged in battle formation, and scattered around their feet a huge collection of swords. The second pit contained more infantry units, alongside cavalry units and chariots pulled by teams of terracotta horses. The third pit revealed high-ranking officers and an elaborate bronze war chariot. The fourth and final pit was empty. In total, the emperor's army numbered eight thousand strong, all lying undiscovered for 2,200 years, buried just a kilometre and a half away from the giant burial mound that houses the mausoleum of emperor Qin Shi Huang.

  As more and more of the figures were painstakingly uncovered, one thing became clear - no two were the same. Built on an industrial scale in various workshops in the late 3rd century BC, each soldier's face had been individually finished off by hand to give it a unique look. Once cast and put together, the soldiers had then been painted to make them look as realistic as possible. Unfortunately, this paint quickly flaked off from the figures when they were unearthed, leading to criticism about the fact that insufficient environmental research had been undertaken before the figures were exposed to the elements.

  A year after the discovery of the army, the Chinese government decided to open up the site to the public. A museum was built, and a protective roof was constructed over the now fully-exposed pits. It wasn't long before tourists began to trickle into the small provincial city of Xi'an, eager to see this amazing sight. Soon word spread around the world, and tourists flew in to see the army in all its glory. The Terracotta Army was officially recognised as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1987. Today, it is one of the most popular tourist attractions in China, and on the occasions when groups of the terracotta soldiers have been allowed out of the country to go on display in national museums around the world, they have attracted hundreds of thousands of visitors.

How staying cool changed the USA

A  The air conditioning unit is one piece of 20th-century technology that is seldom discussed, or even noticed. This is because it is practically invisible when working as it should. However, it has played a role in shaping the modern world almost as great as the motor car or the aeroplane. It is widely known that the USA is easily the biggest per capita consumer of electricity on the planet, but less appreciated is that country's dependence on air conditioning. It has been estimated that US citizens, representing less than 5 per cent of the global population, consume roughly one quarter of all the electricity generated in the world; and fully a third of that goes towards power for air conditioning.

B  Of course, we survived for many millennia without air conditioning, developing all sorts of effective strategies for dealing with the heat. Thousands of years ago, Mesopotamian rulers in what is modern Iraq built double-walled palaces and packed the cavities with imported ice. The street plan of medieval Korcula, a town built by the Venetians on an island off the Dalmatian coast, follows a unique fishbone pattern that maximises shade and channels any available sea breeze. People in northern Afghanistan have for centuries spent the hottest hours of the day on a raised, shaded platform covered with the leaves of palm trees that are periodically dipped in a nearby river - a technique known as evaporative cooling that was also widely practised in 18th-century France.

C  US engineer Willis Carrier (1876-1950) is credited with the invention of air conditioning. His invention was essentially a refrigerator without the insulated box. A cooling gas was compressed until it liquefied, then passed through an expansion valve that caused it to evaporate; the cool air that resulted was distributed by fan. Carrier's cooling gas was ammonia, but these days the less dangerous, non-toxic Freon is used, although the principle underlying the technology is unchanged. In 1902, he installed the world's first air conditioner for a printing company in New York. The firm requested his help because they were struggling to print good quality coloured materials. This was due to the heat and humidity, which were adversely affecting the ink they were printing with.

D  Within a few years, Carrier was pushing the idea of installing air conditioning in public buildings, particularly in theatres which had to close for the summer. Blocks of ice cut out of lakes had been briefly considered to deal with this problem. For example, one US theatre had used four tons a day and a large fan to blow the cold air through pipes towards the audience. This wasn't ideal, as pollution was increasing in nearby lakes, meaning that sometimes the melting ice produced unpleasant smells. Fortunately for theatre-goers, by the 1920s Carrier's air conditioning began to be used in theatres and cinemas, and the general public became aware of this revolutionary new technology.

E  However, it was not until the 1950s that air conditioning was introduced into US homes. In the years following the economic boom of the post-war period, the technology was successfully marketed as a way of quickly and easily controlling the domestic environment. Homeowners demanded it in their millions, and what had originally been considered a luxury rapidly became essential. Soon engineers found a way to install air conditioning in cars; the first air-conditioned vehicles carried immense social status.

F  Since the 1950s, air conditioning has been partly responsible for economic growth in the hot south and south west of the United States, prompting large-scale internal migration to those areas of the country. In many places, it has ended the traditional afternoon sleep, meaning that people take shorter lunch breaks and work longer hours. The widespread availability of air conditioning also meant that office buildings no longer needed to have windows that opened. This made ever taller buildings feasible, which in turn led to high-rise blocks and skyscrapers dominating city skylines around the world.

G  Despite all the improvements since Carrier's day, air conditioning still remains hopelessly inefficient in terms of energy use. In a typical unit, as much as 40 per cent of the energy used is lost in the form of heat. Large numbers of air-conditioning units, blasting out hot air in urban backyards and on the roofs of big buildings, are themselves significant local contributors to warming, and one reason that many cities are often several degrees hotter than their immediate environs. And air conditioning contributes to global as well as local warming. The technological utopia once envisioned for the US by Carrier is in danger of becoming a nightmare. Air conditioning sits at the centre of what physicists call a positive feedback loop, by which they mean that the hotter it gets, the more inclined people are to turn up the dial on their air conditioners, with the result that it gets hotter still. High temperatures can create such a demand for power that local governments are forced to ration it. There have even been blackouts in many countries when people have no electricity at all for hours at a time. Demand for electricity in California has grown rapidly, and the blackouts there have been blamed on the many high-tech companies with headquarters in the state, and the levels of air conditioning these businesses require. It is time that air conditioning was recognised as one of those technologies that, while liberating us, increasingly threatens us too.

Review: Superman on the Couch: What Superheroes Really Tell Us about Ourselves and Our Society

  In his book Superman on the Couch: What Superheroes Really Tell Us about Ourselves and Our Society, author Danny Fingeroth offers a critical work that explores why comic book superheroes have continued to matter so much to so many of us over the past several decades. It also examines why characters such as Batman, Spider-Man and Superman have not only entered into popular consciousness, but, moreover, remained so firmly rooted there. Though Fingeroth doesn't offer much that's really revolutionary or even new in the way of insight or knowledge into what might be best termed 'superhero psychology', he's nevertheless written a sharp and entertaining study that manages to be attractive to general and academic audiences alike, without sacrificing anything in the way of respect for its subject.

  One of the most informative and relevant aspects of the book is the brief history of the standard comic book superhero that Fingeroth provides in the first two chapters. Fingeroth starts off by defining what constitutes a superhero. Defined broadly, 'a hero is someone who rises above his or her fears to achieve something extraordinary ... a hero embodies what we believe is best in ourselves.' Yet, as Fingeroth points out, there is a clearly discernible difference between a 'hero' per se and what we tend to consider to be a 'superhero'. Superheroes, Fingeroth argues, are individuals with fantastic powers, as well as people who fight their battles with amazing technology. They are, in short, heroes who cannot possibly exist in our own world. All superheroes have certain characteristics in common, 'some sort of strength of character, some system of positive values, and a determination, no matter what, to protect those values ... the superhero - more than even the ordinary fictional hero - has to represent the values of the society that produces him.'

  Healthcare professionals have long stressed that patients must take the full course of antibiotics prescribed. However, some experts recently questioned this conventional wisdom in an article in the British Medical Journal (BMJ), suggesting that the advice is not evidence-based and that it impedes the usefulness of antibiotics in the light of bacterial resistance. But other experts have been critical, saying that the call to change established prescribing practice is dangerous, as it is itself unsupported by sufficient evidence.

  In this debate, many professionals actually agree that it is worth reconsidering the duration for which antibiotics need to be taken, and that more clinical trials are needed to specify appropriate doses for different infections. Some consensus is emerging that shorter courses may sometimes be sensible — but more evidence is needed. It has always been unanimously agreed that life-threatening conditions such as tuberculosis merit a longer course of antibiotics to cure the infection and possibly to prevent resistance. But for some common conditions, the recommended course has already been shortened to three days. Public health messages have subtly shifted. For instance, the government agency Public Health England is now telling people to take antibiotics “exactly as prescribed” rather than “completing the course,” while prescribers are being asked to avoid unnecessarily lengthy durations.

  Calls to shorten antibiotic courses and gather more evidence are not new. But until recently, public discussion of the issue was rare. The real controversy now is about what experts should tell the public. The authors of the BMJ article suggest that patients prescribed antibiotics for common bacterial infections could be advised to stop when they feel better. Many of their critics fear that such advice is too subjective, and people will be confused by experts disagreeing or departing from an established message.

  The notion that experts must convey a simple message is based on the assumption that uncertainty creates anxiety, making people unsure of what to believe or how to act. Since being exposed to divergent views increases uncertainty, it seems to follow that experts ought to present a united front. But health communication scholars suggest this is too simplistic, as people manage and respond to uncertainty in different ways. Some people have good reasons to ignore debates among experts, relying instead on familiar routines that shape their beliefs and behavior. Others mistrust displays of excessive confidence, finding that open discussion is more reassuring as it is consistent with their own instincts about knowledge.

  Even where some reduction in uncertainty is desirable, evidence is not a substitute for judgment. Undertaking scientific studies to address complex matters often increases uncertainty as new evidence raises further questions. Clinical trials data generates its own dilemmas of assessment and interpretation for professionals.

  In terms of antibiotic prescribing and similar medical controversies, trials are needed but clinical judgment will still be important. Evidence from trials may be valuable but it must be put in the context of other evidence and practical objectives. The same principle applies to most issues that experts investigate, from gender differences to trade deficits.

  So what of the future? In the case of antibiotic courses, it is unreasonable to expect that new evidence will automatically resolve current uncertainties. Science cannot meet such undue expectations. But this is only a problem in a culture where people assume medical recommendations will be based on unshakeable proof, and where experts cultivate that impression. We see a similar pattern to this on other issues, such as climate change, where science is invoked to justify particular interventions to the public.

  Tensions around the public role of science arise partly from the belief that the cultural credibility of expertise rests on communicating information within a framework of consensus. The result of this is that credibility becomes strained whenever new knowledge seems to challenge current consensus. This can deflect the spotlight away from more urgent practical needs, particularly in the field of health communication. For example, reactions to a recent turnabout in diet advice, which now targets excess sugar rather than fat, have diverted attention away from the more pressing need to reduce levels of obesity.

  But if conflicting or inconclusive evidence from new science is taken to be the norm rather than the exception, uncertainty wouldn't be a problem to fear or eliminate. Similar points have been made in relation to new technologies of screening and diagnostic testing, where the evidence provided is often ambiguous.

  Promising consensus as derived from scientific evidence is a perilous principle on which to found meaningful engagement between experts and the public. Rather than promising the impossible, we should be trying to facilitate improved ways of appraising and coping with entirely normal uncertainties and reasons for disagreement.
